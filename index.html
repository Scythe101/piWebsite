PI.

Pi is a mathematical constant in use all over math, engineering, and science

...but it's so much more.

Pi has a long, interesting history.

Let's jump in.

Chapter 1: The Egyptians and Babylonians

The Babylonians used a rough approximation of Pi when they made a formula (\(A=3r^2\)) for calculating the area of a circle. They gave Pi a value of 3. A Babylonian stone tablet dating back to around 1900 BCE found a slightly more accurate approximation of 3.125 by relating the length of the circumference to the perimiter of an inscribed hexagon. The Egyptians took a different approach. Their formula for finding a circle used 3.1604, a different approximation for Pi. But soon, these approximations would soon be put to shame by a Greek mathematician.

Chapter 2: Polygon Approximation

Archimedes, a Greek mathematician, found an algorithm for calculating the value of Pi in around 250 BCE. He did this by using the method of exhaustion, which is an approach used to calculate the area or volume of a shape that is difficult to calculate directly. Archimedes would find the perimeters of polygons inscribed (inside of) and circumscribed (containing) a circle, and this would give the upper and lower boundaries of pi. Archimedes did this until he reached a 96-sided polygon, giving \(3.1408\lt\pi\lt3.1429\).

In around 150 CE, a Greek-Roman scientist named Ptolemy gave \(\pi\) a value of \(3.1416\). Using the method of exhaustion, eventually mathematicians reached a record of 39 digits of \(\pi\). In ancient China, \(\pi\) ranged from \(3.1547\), to \(\sqrt 10\), and \(142 \over 45\).


In around 265 CE, Liu Hui created a different polygon approximation algorithm to calculate Pi, and used a 3,072-sided polygon to calculate a value of \(3.1416\). He later found a more efficient algorithm for calculating \(\pi\), and used a 96-sided polygon to find a value of \(3.14\). Another Chinese mathematician, Zu Chongzhi, calculated that \(3.1415926\lt\pi\lt3.1415927\). Using Liu Hui's algorithm, and a 12,288-sided polygon, he found 2 approximations of \(\pi\): \(355\over113\) and \(22\over7\). The former would be the most accurate approximation of \(\pi\) for centuries.


More people would continue to use similar methods, with increasing side counts, until Austrian astronomer Christoph Grienberger found 38 digits of \(\pi\) in 1630, using a whopping \(10^{40}\)-sided polygon.


Chapter 3: Infinite Series

In the 16th and 17th centuries, \(\pi\) calculation was forever changed by the use of infinite series techniques. Infinite series are when you find the sum of the terms in an infinite sequence. This allowed people to compute \(\pi\) to much more precision than any of the polygon approximation algorithms. The idea of using an infinite series to compute \(\pi\) started in the 14th or 15th century, and a written description for this technique was outlined in 1500 CE. It was originally proofless, but then they were added in a later work in around 1530 CE. Viète's formula was published by François Viète in 1593, and it uses an infinite product, rather than an infinite sum, which is commonly used in \(\pi\) calculations. In 1655, John Wallis published an infinite product for \(\pi\): \(\pi/2=(2/1\times2/3)\times(4/3\times4/5)\times(6/5\times6/7)...\).


And then came along Isaac Newton who invented calculus. This led to many more infinite series that approximated \(\pi\). Isaac Newton himself used a arcsine series to find a 15-digit approximation of \(\pi\).\ In 1671, James Gregory and later, Gottfried, in 1673, both independantly discovered the Taylor series expansion, a way to represent a function as an infinite sum, for arctangent: arctan \(z=z-{z^3\over3}+{z^5\over5}+{z^7\over7}...\). This series equals \(\pi\over4\) when \(z=1\). Unfortunately, the convergence rate is extremely slow, and needed to be speeded up. In 1699, Abraham Sharp, an English mathematician, used \(z=1\over\sqrt3\) to find \(\pi\) to 71 digits.


For the first time, 100 digits of \(\pi\) were calculated in 1706 by John Machin using a much faster algorithm: \({\pi\over4}=4\)arctan\({1\over{5}}-\)arctan\(1\over239\) Later, more people would use variants of this algorithm (called Machin-like formulae), which were used to calculate more digits of \(\pi\). Isaac Newton would later improve upon previous algorithms, but his results were never published: arctan \(x={x\over{1+x^2}}+{2\over{3}}\times{x^3\over{(1+x^2)^2}}+{{{2\times4}\over{3\times5}}\times{{x^5}\over{(1+x^2)^3}}}+...\). In his 1755 differential calculus textbook, Euler would later popularize this series, and improve upon it, being able to calculate 20 digits of \(\pi\) in 20 minutes.


A new record of 200 digits of \(\pi\) was calculated in 1853 by Zacharias Dase. This record would later be beaten by William Shanks, a British scientist. He calculated 607 digits, but unfortunately, he made a mistake in the 528th digit, which messed up all following digits. He attempted to add 100 digits 20 years later, but due to his grave error, those were false as well.


Some infinite series were prioritized because they were more efficient and took less time to converge to \(\pi\). The less the time it takes for an infinite series to converge, the less computations it takes to find \(\pi\).

Chapter 4: \(\pi\)'s Adoption

Originally, \(\pi\) was used as a symbol for one half the circle's circumference. \(\pi\) was first used in the way we use it now by William Jones in 1706. Although, its usage in that way did not truly take off until Euler started using it in his essays. Depending on the essay, the value of \(\pi\) could be \(3.14...\) or \(6.28\), depending on which value suited the calculations best.

\(e^{i\pi}+1=0\)

Chapter 5: Modern Era

The widespread adoption of computers in the 20th century led to breakthroughs in the values of \(\pi\) that could be computed. In 1949, John Wrench and Levi Smith found 1,120 digits, by using only a desk calculator. George Reitwiesner and John von Neumann used an arctan infinite series to find 2,037 digits within the same year by using the ENIAC computer. The record kept being broken repeatedly, but they all still used the same method (infinite arctan series). Milestones include: 10,000 digits in 1958, 100,000 digits in 1961, and 1 million digits in 1973.


In 1980, many advances were made in computers that made calculating \(\pi\) even faster. New iterative algorithms were discorvered that were much faster at calculating \(\pi\) than infinite series. Essentially, iterative algorithms are where subsequent calculations are based on the previous calculation. Furthermore, new, much faster multiplication algorithms were developed that allowed for quicker calculations.


Iterative algorithms were a big deal because, unlike infinite series, which add a digit for each new term, iterative algorithms multiply the amount of digits at each step. These algorithms were published in 1975-1976 by Eugene Salamin and Richard Brent. Funnily enough, this was actually discovered >160 years prior by Carl Friedrich Gauss. The Gauss-Legendre algorithm converges extremely fast, giving 45 million correct \(\pi\) digits in only 25 iterations. John and Peter Borwein made another algorithm which was even faster; the algorithm quadrupled the number of digits in each iteration, and a later one quintupled the number of digits in each iteration. A Japanese mathematician, Yasumasa Kanada, set multiple records for \(\pi\) digits calculated between 1995 and 2002.


Other, more fun, ways of calculating \(\pi\) are the Monte Carlo methods, which take the results of random results and create approximations of \(\pi\). Chapter 6: \(\pi\) Today

Today, \(\pi\) is still used all over the world in many scientific and mathematical formulae, such as the formula for the area of a circle. People are still calculating way more digits of \(\pi\) than we could ever need, just to try to break the record. A symbol with many origins, \(\pi\) is arguably one of the most important constants in mathematics, and it has revolutionized math and science.
